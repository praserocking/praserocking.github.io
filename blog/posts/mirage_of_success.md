# The Mirage of Success: Why We Need to Throw Away the Yardstick

### **The Mirage of Borrowed Success**
Success, when defined by someone else, is an abstract concept. It operates like a mirage. It gives you the comforting hope of an oasis while you are wandering aimlessly through a desert, but when you finally reach it, there's nothing there to quench your thirst. 

We often fall into the trap of believing that if we just do what the rest of the world considers "successful," we will finally arrive at happiness. But chasing this borrowed definition rarely leads to fulfillment; instead, it becomes little more than a tool for stroking our own egos or a yardstick to continuously measure ourselves against others. 

I believe the true concept of success can only be defined by the individual. To find it, you have to mentally travel back to the times when you were growing up. What did you really want to do? What made you feel genuinely happy and contented? That feeling is probably your own, authentic version of success. Your younger self only thought about what brought pure joy, completely unburdened by the world's manufactured picture of what a successful life is supposed to look like.

### **The Differing Yardsticks of Civilization**
To understand how arbitrary our definition of success really is, you only need to look at close-knit communities across the globe. Reflecting on my own experiences, I think about the Baduga people, or the indigenous tribes in the Andaman Islands. To the modern, industrialized world, their lifestyle might seem "savage" or disconnected from our standard metrics of progress. Yet, they are undeniably content. When they watch outsiders cross their land in big 4x4s, they don't look at the machinery and suddenly feel unsuccessful. They don't envy it. Their yardstick for a good life is simply built on a completely different scale. 

I saw the exact same phenomenon at the edge of the world during a trip to Iceland. In the remote, northern parts of the country, people live isolated but incredibly fulfilled lives without any of the urban luxuries I own. Their days consist of the necessary work required for survival, followed by finding profound peace in the absolute simplest of things—eating a basic meal while watching an Arctic tern dive for a fish. 

It makes you realize a harsh truth: **life is inherently simple and peaceful. It is us who tend to complicate it.** We exhaust ourselves chasing unimportant things, pursuing milestones that don't actually bring us joy, simply because someone else defined them as the ultimate metric of success. 

### **When the Intermediary Became the Master**
Whenever I think about the modern concept of money, a specific scene from the movie *The Big Short* always stands out. It’s the scene where Selena Gomez sits at a casino table and explains the basketball fallacy—how people place bets on other people's bets, turning a straightforward $10 million investment into billions of dollars of leveraged exposure. 

It’s a *fugazi*. It's an illusion. 

If we look back at the origins of human commerce, the concept of money was invented for a very practical, grounded reason: it was simply an intermediary for the barter system. It was a tool created to make exchanging real, tangible goods easier. 

But somewhere along the line, we corrupted the system. People started trading on promises built *on* that intermediary. We layered derivatives, debt, and bets upon bets, and in doing so, we completely flipped the natural hierarchy. **The intermediary became the master, and the real master became the derived stuff from the system.** We forgot that money was supposed to be a tool to facilitate life, and instead made accumulating the tool the entire point of living. 

### **The Infinite Hoard and the Wealth Paradox**
The trap of the modern financial system is that people don't just accumulate money to a certain, comfortable extent—they chase it to the end of natural numbers, and the pursuit never actually stops. 

Living here in India, this phenomenon takes on an even stranger cultural twist. Society actively applauds the person who has hoarded massive amounts of wealth in their accounts while living an ultra-austere life. We’ve all heard the stories: society reveres the person sitting on a 10+ crore net worth who still drives a simple Honda City and lives in a modest 2BHK. 

But it begs a string of uncomfortable questions: Isn't the money you earn meant to be enjoyed in the reality of your daily life? Who exactly are they hoarding it for? What is the actual purpose of their life? 

If all that person genuinely desired was the simple Honda City and the quiet 2BHK, why sacrifice so much time and life energy to hoard the excess wealth? When you really think about it, it all circles back to the very first point: **the yardstick of the community**. They aren't accumulating capital for its utility; they are accumulating it because their community's metric of success demands a high score, even if that score is never actually spent. 

### **Throwing Away the Yardstick**
Ultimately, all I strive for now is to not be consumed by the synthetic tranches of society. 

> *"Live a life you will remember."* — Avicii

Not a life your neighbors will remember. Not a life your parents will remember, or one that society will politely applaud from the sidelines. Be true to yourself and live something that is genuinely worth your limited, irreplaceable time. 

Before spending a lifetime on any pursuit, we have to pause and ask the hard question: *Is this what I really want to do with my life?* Far too often, we spend the best of our living years grinding away at something we never actually wanted to do. 

It is time to throw the societal yardstick away. Stop measuring your progress against the community's arbitrary scorecard. Instead, measure your choices against what your younger, uncorrupted self wanted to do, and live toward that. It makes all the difference.
